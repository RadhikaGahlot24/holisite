ABOUT US

Holi, the Festival of Colors, is a vibrant and joyous Hindu festival primarily celebrated in India and Nepal, marking the arrival of spring and the triumph of good over evil. It usually takes place in March, on the full moon day of the Hindu month of Phalguna. The festival is rooted in several myths, notably the story of Prahlad and the demon king Hiranyakashipu, symbolizing the victory of devotion and righteousness. Holi is most famous for the playful throwing of colored powders and water, a tradition that spreads joy and unity among people of all ages and backgrounds. The celebrations also include bonfires known as Holika Dahan, symbolizing the burning of evil, and the preparation of special foods like gujiya and thandai.

The most iconic tradition is the playful throwing of brightly colored powders (gulal) at friends, family, and even strangers, filling the air with vivid hues. This joyful activity takes place in streets, parks, and open spaces, with people laughing and dancing together. The night before Holi, people gather around bonfires in a ritual known as Holika Dahan, symbolizing the destruction of evil, commemorating the mythological story of Holika and Prahlad. Music, dance, and traditional songs (Holi Geet) are also an integral part of the celebration, creating a festive atmosphere. Special foods like gujiya (sweet pastries) and thandai (a milk-based drink) are enjoyed, adding to the indulgence of the festival. Holi is also a time for visiting friends and family, strengthening social bonds and spreading joy.

FAMOUS PLACES

Vrindavan Holi

Vrindavan is famous for Holi because it is considered the childhood home of Lord Krishna, where many of the legendary Holi stories are associated, making the celebrations in Vrindavan particularly significant and traditional, with unique customs like "Phoolwali Holi" (flower-based Holi) and a vibrant display of colors across the city; essentially, experiencing Holi in Vrindavan is seen as a way to relive the playful Holi stories of Krishna and radha.

Beawer Badshah Mela

Beawar in Rajasthan is famous for its Badshah ki Sawari, a ceremonial procession that takes place during Holi and is a key highlight of the festival
During the procession, a man dressed as the emperor rides around the town in a rath, throwing color powder and distributing packets of gulal to the people. The procession ends at the sub divisional magistrate office, where the emperor and the SDM play a symbolic game of throwing color powder.

Pushkar Holi

Pushkar Holi is famous for its unique blend of tradition and celebration.
Pushkar is a holy city with a rich religious and cultural heritage. The festival blends the city's culture with the spirit of Holi.
The festival includes traditional rituals like prayers at the Brahma temple and a procession carrying the deity of Lord Krishna.
The festival is known for its vibrant colors, music, and dancing. People throw colored powder into the air and cover each other in it.
